Horizons – Near and Far

 

Most painters of nature and “the real” present you with a fait accompli: that is, they provide you with a window or stage where you, the viewer, do not have many options. Your vision is directed to where the artist wants you to look.

 

But in the recent paintings by Joan Branca of Long Island’s north shore, the artist gives your eyes permission to wander. Should we look at the rocks on the shore near us? Or perhaps the tidal pools just beyond? We are being subtly led across the north shore’s inlet or harbor to the light on the other shore. What are those white patches? Are there people over there looking at us on this sunny summer day?

 

Joan Branca provides all of this and a sense of mystery too. Are those far clouds storm clouds? Is that heat lightning over the sound?

 

Like the nineteenth century lumininists, she provides a definite sense of place and mood, but she also leaves the viewer the freedom to interpret the impressionist brushstrokes. She lets the viewer complete the picture and arrive at his own interpretation of the scene, his own sense of mood. In this Joan Branca shows a complete contemporary sensibility that repays every viewing of her paintings with new insights upon each encounter.

 

 

John Remsen

Curator  Coastal Scenes Exhibition
